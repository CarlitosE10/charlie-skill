# MOAT Taxonomy

A **moat** is a structural advantage that protects a company's economic returns (excess returns above cost of capital) from competition over time. Charlie identifies which of the five canonical moat types — if any — apply, and rates the moat's overall strength and durability.

This taxonomy follows Pat Dorsey / Morningstar's framework, which has become the most widely adopted model for moat analysis.

---

## The five moat types

### 1. Intangible Assets

**What it is:** Brands, patents, regulatory licenses, government franchises — things that competitors cannot replicate because they're legally protected, hard-won, or rooted in customer perception.

**Quantitative fingerprints:**
- High and stable gross margins (brand pricing power)
- High R&D intensity translated into a defensible patent portfolio (pharma, semiconductors)
- Regulatory scarcity (FDA approval, broadcast spectrum, casino licenses)

**Examples:** Coca-Cola (brand), Pfizer (patents), Visa/Mastercard (regulatory + network), Moody's (regulatory franchise).

**Common pitfalls in identification:**
- A "famous" brand isn't necessarily a moat — does it command pricing premium? (Compare Apple → yes; Sony → not really.)
- Patents expire. R&D intensity matters because it sustains the patent pipeline.

---

### 2. Switching Costs

**What it is:** Customers face real cost (time, money, risk, retraining, integration) to leave for a competitor.

**Quantitative fingerprints:**
- High operating margin stability (customers don't churn)
- High recurring / subscription revenue mix
- Low customer acquisition cost relative to lifetime value

**Examples:** Salesforce, Microsoft Office, SAP, Bloomberg Terminal, Autodesk, medical device companies (surgeons trained on a specific implant).

**Common pitfalls:**
- "Sticky" doesn't mean "switching cost." A subscription you can cancel any month with no consequence isn't a real switching cost.
- The cost must be borne by the *customer*, not just by the vendor.

---

### 3. Network Effects

**What it is:** Each new user makes the product more valuable to every existing user. The network itself is the product.

**Quantitative fingerprints:**
- Operating margin expansion as the network grows (winner-take-most economics)
- Revenue concentration in market leaders
- Very high gross margins (digital marginal cost)

**Examples:** Visa/Mastercard (two-sided), Meta/Google (advertiser ↔ user), Airbnb (host ↔ guest), eBay, LinkedIn, X.

**Common pitfalls:**
- Network effects can be local (Uber competes city-by-city) or global (Visa competes globally). Local networks are weaker.
- Multi-homing erodes network effects. (Drivers can run Uber + Lyft simultaneously.)

---

### 4. Cost Advantages

**What it is:** Structural ability to produce or deliver at lower cost than competitors — through scale, location, process know-how, or unique input access.

**Quantitative fingerprints:**
- Low and stable operating costs as % of revenue
- High inventory turnover (process efficiency)
- Low SG&A intensity vs peers

**Examples:** Walmart and Costco (scale-driven distribution + buying power), Geico (direct-to-consumer cost structure), low-cost natural resource producers (oil sands, certain copper mines).

**Common pitfalls:**
- Cost advantage from scale alone can be replicated by other large players. The advantage must be structural — protected by something more than just being big.
- Cost advantages erode when input prices normalize.

---

### 5. Efficient Scale

**What it is:** A market is large enough to support only one or a few profitable players. New entry would push everyone below break-even.

**Quantitative fingerprints:**
- Stable, oligopolistic market structure
- Persistent ROIC well above cost of capital despite mature growth
- Limited new entrants over a 10-year+ window

**Examples:** Pipeline companies (one pipeline serves the corridor), railroad networks, regional airports, certain regional utilities.

**Common pitfalls:**
- This is often confused with "monopoly." Efficient scale isn't a legal monopoly — it's a structural one driven by the size of the addressable market.

---

## Quantitative anchors → MOAT strength

When Charlie reads the proxies, this is the rough mapping:

| Proxy reading | Implication |
|---|---|
| Gross margin avg > 50% AND stability cv < 0.10 | Strong intangibles or switching costs likely |
| Operating margin avg > 25% AND stability cv < 0.15 | Durable competitive advantage of *some* type |
| ROIC avg > 20% AND stability cv < 0.20 | Strong moat; capital is being deployed at high returns |
| ROIC avg < 10% | Weak / no moat — returns are at or below cost of capital |
| R&D intensity > 10% AND consistent | Likely intangible-asset moat (R&D-driven) |
| CAPEX intensity > 15% AND high market share | Likely cost-advantage or efficient-scale moat |
| Revenue 3y CAGR > sector average | Moat is translating into above-sector growth |

---

## MOAT durability

After identifying *what* moat type applies, judge *how long* it's likely to remain effective:

- **<5 years (weak):** Moat is real today but visibly eroding. Disruption is approaching.
- **5–10 years (neutral):** Moat is intact for the foreseeable future, but specific threats exist.
- **10–20 years (strong):** Moat is deeply rooted; no clear path to erosion is visible.
- **>20 years (very_strong):** Multi-decade moat (e.g., Coca-Cola brand, Visa network).

---

## Calibration discipline

- Most companies have **no moat**. Default rating is "neutral" or "weak" unless evidence is unambiguous.
- Never assign **very_strong** unless you can name the specific moat type *and* point to durability evidence.
- The combination matters: a company with **multiple moat types** (intangibles + switching costs + network effects, e.g., Visa) is materially more durable than one with a single moat type.
