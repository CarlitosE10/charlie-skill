# Buffett 5-Dimension Framework

Five lenses for understanding the durable qualitative character of a business. The synthesis layer applies each one when generating the qualitative report. The framework is associated with Warren Buffett's investment philosophy as articulated through the Berkshire Hathaway shareholder letters, and it overlaps substantially with Porter's Five Forces — applied at the firm level rather than the industry level.

---

## 1. Product

**The question:** *What does the company sell, and is what it sells likely to be wanted in 10–20 years?*

**Sub-questions:**
- What is the core product or service? (One sentence.)
- How concentrated is revenue across products? (One product = single-point-of-failure; many products = diversified but possibly unfocused.)
- Is the product a commodity or differentiated?
- Does the company have pricing power — can it raise prices without losing volume?
- How easy is substitution? (Substitutes, alternatives, "do-nothing" alternatives all count.)

**Calibration anchors:**
- **Pricing power = strong**: customers pay materially above cost, and price hikes are absorbed (e.g., consumer staples leaders, dominant software platforms, branded luxury).
- **Pricing power = neutral**: pricing roughly tracks input costs; customers shop around but don't immediately defect.
- **Pricing power = weak**: pricing is set by the market; the company is a price-taker (e.g., commodity producers, undifferentiated retail).
- **Substitutability = low**: switching to a substitute imposes real cost or workflow disruption.
- **Substitutability = high**: substitutes exist with comparable utility and price.

## 2. Sector

**The question:** *Is the sector growing, mature, or declining? And how brutal is competition inside it?*

**Sub-questions:**
- Sector maturity stage: emerging, growth, mature, declining.
- Barriers to entry: regulatory, capital, scale, network, brand, IP.
- Disruption risk: is a new technology, regulation, or business model threatening the sector?
- Sector momentum: is the sector ETF outperforming or underperforming the broader market?

**Calibration anchors:**
- **Barriers high**: airlines (capital + regulation), utilities (regulation + capital), branded pharma (IP + regulation), social networks (network effects).
- **Barriers low**: restaurants, basic retail, consulting, most app development.
- **Disruption risk high**: incumbent industries facing AI/software/regulatory change (e.g., legacy automakers vs EVs, legacy media vs streaming, fossil fuel power vs renewables).
- **Sector outlook**: anchor on the deterministic 3m / YTD ETF return + geopolitical risk presence.

---

## 3. Customer

**The question:** *Who are the customers, how concentrated are they, and how locked-in?*

**Sub-questions:**
- B2B or B2C? Mixed?
- Customer concentration: does any one customer (or a small group) represent >10% of revenue?
- Switching costs: how much pain or cost does a customer incur if they leave?
- Customer loyalty / repeat purchase rate.

**Calibration anchors:**
- **Concentration very_high**: a single customer is >25% of revenue (e.g., some defense contractors, semi-equipment makers selling to TSMC).
- **Concentration moderate**: top 5 customers are 20–40% combined.
- **Concentration low**: highly diversified consumer base, no single customer matters.
- **Switching costs strong**: enterprise software with deep integrations; medical devices in trained workflows; banking primary accounts.
- **Switching costs weak**: SaaS with month-to-month billing; commodities; basic e-commerce.

---

## 4. Supplier

**The question:** *Who feeds the business, and how vulnerable is the supply chain?*

**Sub-questions:**
- Supplier concentration: does the company depend on one or two critical suppliers?
- Negotiating power: who has leverage — the company or the supplier?
- Critical inputs: which inputs would, if disrupted, halt operations?
- Supply-chain risk: geographic concentration, regulatory exposure, single-source components.

**Calibration anchors:**
- **Supplier concentration very_high**: depends on a single foundry (TSMC), a single rare-earth source, a single key software API.
- **Supplier concentration low**: many interchangeable suppliers, commodity inputs.
- **Negotiating power strong** (for the company): the company is large enough to dictate terms (Walmart, Amazon, Apple to most of its suppliers).
- **Critical inputs**: name them concretely. Examples — "leading-edge silicon wafers," "AWS cloud capacity," "lithium," "qualified pilots," "natural gas at Henry Hub price."

---

## 5. Macro

**The question:** *What macro forces shape the company's prospects?*

**Sub-questions:**
- Interest rate sensitivity: do higher rates help, hurt, or barely affect the business? (Banks benefit from steep curves; capital-intensive utilities/REITs are penalized.)
- FX sensitivity: where is revenue / cost denominated? Is the company net long or net short the dollar?
- Regulatory environment: is the regulator hostile, neutral, or supportive?
- Cyclicality: how does the business behave across the economic cycle? Defensive, neutral, cyclical, highly cyclical.
- Inflation sensitivity: can the company pass through cost increases?

**Calibration anchors:**
- **Defensive sectors**: Consumer Defensive, Healthcare, Utilities — earnings hold up in recessions.
- **Cyclical sectors**: Consumer Cyclical, Industrials, Materials, Financials, Real Estate — earnings track the cycle.
- **Highly cyclical**: Energy — earnings are driven by commodity prices.
- **Rate-sensitive winners**: banks (NIM expansion in steep curves), insurance.
- **Rate-sensitive losers**: REITs, utilities, long-duration tech (DCF discount rate compression).

## How to use this framework

When synthesizing the qualitative report:

1. **Anchor each judgment to a fact.** If you say "pricing power is strong," cite something — a margin trend, a market position, a customer-concentration data point, or a description detail from the business summary.
2. **Don't over-rate.** Most ratings should land near "neutral" unless evidence is unambiguous. Reserve "very_strong" / "very_weak" for cases where the evidence is overwhelming.
3. **Remember the goal.** The purpose is to surface qualitative texture that complements numerical analysis. Avoid duplicating ratio-driven conclusions; focus on the durability and character of the business.
4. **Stay non-prescriptive.** Never say "buy", "sell", "hold", or "investment opportunity." Business quality assessment is the output; investment decisions belong to the consumer of this report.
